You are helping classify commercially successful songs for a portfolio data project.

Project title:
Heartbroken, Hopeful, or Happy? An AI-Assisted Analysis of Themes in 2025’s Highest-Performing Songs

Research question:
What themes, emotions, message orientations, and value signals appear most often among 2025’s highest-performing songs across selected Billboard year-end charts?

Important context:
- The dataset contains songs from selected Billboard 2025 year-end charts.
- Some songs may appear on more than one chart.
- Classify each unique song once.
- Use chart appearances only as context, not as the basis for classification.
- Do not reproduce, quote, or output copyrighted lyrics.
- You may use song-level understanding, public song knowledge, lyric-level understanding, artist context, reviews, annotations, interviews, and widely known interpretations.
- Do not store, reproduce, quote, or return full lyrics.
- If lyric-level evidence is available, use it to inform the classification without quoting lyrics.
- If the song is ambiguous, information is limited, or the classification requires guesswork, mark human_review_needed as Yes.
- Do not overclaim psychological impact on listeners.
- Do not assume a sad song is harmful.
- Do not assume an upbeat song is positive or constructive.
- Classify the song’s apparent message and emotional narrative, not just its sonic mood.
- Choose only from the allowed category values below.
- Return CSV format only.
- Preserve the input unique_song_id, song_title, and artist exactly.

Core classification principles:
- Self-awareness is not the same as growth.
- Confession is not the same as accountability.
- Therapy framing is not the same as healing.
- Sadness is not the same as resilience.
- Flexing success is not the same as self-worth.
- Ego is not the same as grounded confidence.
- Wealth/status is not the same as self-worth.
- Describing dysfunction is not the same as criticizing it.
- A song can be clever, funny, successful, vulnerable, or self-aware while still signaling instability, avoidance, revenge, materialism, ego, or emotional chaos.
- A song can be emotionally negative but constructively reflective.
- A song can be emotionally positive but still carry an escapist or concerning message.

Theme definitions:
- Love & Attachment: Love, devotion, loyalty, emotional attachment, romantic commitment, or longing for secure connection.
- Heartbreak & Longing: Breakups, romantic grief, rejection, missing someone, loss, or unresolved longing.
- Sex & Desire: Sexual desire, lust, attraction, seduction, sexual pursuit, or intimate physical longing.
- Celebration & Escape: Partying, dancing, fun, leisure, carefree enjoyment, release, or pleasure as escape.
- Status & Success: Money, fame, luxury, achievement, popularity, image, career success, or social rank.
- Confidence & Power: Swagger, dominance, boldness, self-assertion, superiority, ego, or being in control.
- Conflict & Revenge: Diss tracks, retaliation, enemies, betrayal, confrontation, resentment, rebellion, or fighting back.
- Pain & Avoidance: Emotional shutdown, denial, numbness, despair, detachment, intoxication-as-coping, or refusing to face pain.
- Identity Crisis: Confusion, fractured selfhood, insecurity, instability, self-questioning, contradiction, or inner conflict.
- Healing & Resilience: Actual recovery, perseverance, hope, repair, growth, becoming better, or moving through pain constructively.
- Survival & Struggle: Hardship, poverty, systemic pressure, injustice, community struggle, endurance, or survival under difficult conditions.
- Other: Use only when none of the categories fit.

Theme selection rules:
- Choose the dominant narrative as primary_theme.
- Choose a secondary_theme only if it adds a clearly meaningful second dimension.
- Use No Secondary Theme if the secondary theme is weak, speculative, or redundant.
- Do not choose Healing & Resilience unless the song clearly moves toward recovery, perseverance, repair, hope, or constructive endurance.
- Do not choose Identity Crisis for ordinary sadness or heartbreak unless the song is specifically about confusion, fractured identity, contradiction, or inner instability.
- Do not choose Confidence & Power when the song is mainly about material achievement; use Status & Success.
- Do not choose Status & Success when the song is mainly about ego, dominance, swagger, or superiority; use Confidence & Power.
- Do not choose Love & Attachment for obsession, clinging, toxicity, or inability to move on unless genuine love/attachment is the dominant point.

Emotion rules:
- Joy: happiness, celebration, delight, lightness.
- Sadness: grief, sorrow, regret, emotional pain.
- Longing: yearning, missing someone, unresolved desire for connection.
- Anger: resentment, rage, bitterness, hostility.
- Confidence: swagger, self-assurance, dominance, pride.
- Anxiety: fear, pressure, unease, nervousness, instability.
- Desire: lust, attraction, sexual/romantic craving.
- Numbness: emotional shutdown, detachment, emptiness, denial.
- Hope: optimism, faith in improvement, expectation of healing.
- Mixed: multiple emotions are equally central or the emotional tone is contradictory.
- Anxiety does not replace Identity Crisis. Anxiety is a felt emotion; Identity Crisis is a theme about selfhood, confusion, or inner contradiction.

emotional_valence:
- -2 = strongly negative emotional tone
- -1 = somewhat negative emotional tone
- 0 = mixed or neutral emotional tone
- 1 = somewhat positive emotional tone
- 2 = strongly positive emotional tone

Valence rules:
- Judge the emotional world of the song, not just the beat.
- Upbeat production does not automatically mean positive valence.
- Humor, swagger, catchiness, or commercial success can coexist with negative valence.
- A chaotic or self-destructive song can have high energy but negative valence.
- A sad song can have negative valence even if it is thoughtful or well-written.

Allowed values for agency:
- Low
- Mixed
- High

Agency definitions:
- Low: The narrator seems trapped, powerless, passive, overwhelmed, or controlled by circumstances.
- Mixed: The narrator shows both struggle and some control, movement, or self-awareness.
- High: The narrator seems decisive, empowered, assertive, forceful, resilient, dominant, or in control.

Agency rules:
- Destructive confidence can still be High agency.
- Emotional chaos with some self-awareness is usually Mixed agency.
- Being sad does not automatically mean Low agency.

Relationship focus definitions:

- New Love: Early-stage romance or romantic discovery.

- Separation: Breakup, distance, divorce, or relationship ending.

Relationship focus rules:

- Do not use Stable Love for obsession, clinging, unresolved attachment, or inability to move on.
- If the song is sexual but not emotionally relational, use Desire.

Allowed values for message_orientation:
- Encouraging
- Reflective
- Mixed
- Escapist
- Concerning

Message orientation definitions:
- Encouraging: The song clearly points toward healthy love, hope, repair, maturity, responsibility, growth, perseverance, or constructive resilience.
- Reflective: The song describes pain, dysfunction, contradiction, injustice, or chaos with self-awareness, critique, satire, observation, or caution. Reflective does not mean positive.
- Mixed: The song contains both constructive and concerning elements, or the message depends heavily on interpretation.
- Escapist: The song centers pleasure, partying, sex, luxury, intoxication, distraction, or release without much critique or constructive movement.
- Concerning: The song centers or normalizes revenge, despair, toxic relationships, emotional chaos, objectification, destructive coping, harmful pride, or toxic dominance.

Message orientation rules:
- Do not classify a song as Encouraging just because it is vulnerable, confessional, therapy-framed, sad, or self-aware.
- Therapy-style framing alone does not make a song Reflective or Encouraging.
- Reflective songs should usually receive message_score = 0 unless they clearly move toward repair or growth.
- Escapist songs can sound joyful but still have message_score = -1 or 0.
- Concerning songs may still be clever, satirical, popular, or emotionally honest.

message_score:
- -2 = strongly concerning message
- -1 = somewhat concerning, escapist, unstable, self-focused, destructive, or unresolved message
- 0 = mixed, descriptive, reflective, ambiguous, or neutral message
- 1 = somewhat constructive or encouraging message
- 2 = strongly constructive or encouraging message

Message score alignment rules:
- Encouraging should usually be 1 or 2.
- Reflective should usually be 0, unless the song clearly points toward repair, change, responsibility, or hope.
- Mixed should usually be 0.
- Escapist should usually be -1 or 0.
- Concerning should usually be -1 or -2.
- Use 2 only when the song clearly emphasizes repair, healthy commitment, responsibility, perseverance, or positive change.
- Use 1 when the message is mostly constructive but not deeply developed.
- Use 0 when the song is self-aware or reflective but does not clearly move toward growth.
- Use -1 when the song emphasizes avoidance, instability, revenge, materialism, ego, emotional chaos, excess, or destructive coping.
- Use -2 when the song strongly endorses harm, revenge, toxicity, despair, humiliation, or destructive behavior.
- If you deviate from these defaults, the rationale must make the reason clear.

Value signal definition:
value_signal measures the main value, behavior, coping pattern, or worldview the song appears to normalize, celebrate, wrestle with, or point toward.

Value signal definitions:
- Connection: Love, commitment, loyalty, secure attachment, devotion, or meaningful relational bond.
- Resilience: Endurance with hope, perseverance, strength through hardship, or continuing despite pain.
- Growth: Accountability, maturity, repair, responsibility, changed behavior, or actual personal development.
- Self-Worth: Grounded dignity, self-respect, boundaries, or healthy self-regard.
- Joy/Pleasure: Fun, sensuality, enjoyment, desire, indulgence, celebration, or pleasure as a central value.
- Escape: Release, distraction, partying, intoxication, freedom from pressure, or avoiding reality through pleasure.
- Status: Money, luxury, fame, material success, popularity, social rank, image, or achievement as central value.
- Ego/Power: Dominance, superiority, swagger, control, being untouchable, pride, forcefulness, or ego as central value.
- Revenge: Retaliation, enemies, payback, dominance through conflict, resentment, or vindication.
- Avoidance: Denial, numbness, emotional shutdown, refusing to confront pain, or keeping distance from vulnerability.
- Instability: Chaos, contradiction, confusion, fractured identity, spiraling, unresolved dysfunction, or emotional volatility.
- Survival: Enduring hardship, poverty, systemic pressure, dangerous conditions, or struggle as a way of life.
- Other: Use only when none of the categories fit.

Strict value signal rules:
- Do not choose Growth unless the narrator clearly accepts responsibility, changes, repairs harm, matures, or moves toward healthier behavior.
- Do not choose Resilience just because the narrator is suffering.
- Do not choose Self-Worth for ego, flexing, dominance, revenge, status-seeking, or superiority.
- Do not choose Connection for obsession, clinging, possession, toxicity, or inability to move on.
- Do not choose Joy/Pleasure for destructive escape if avoidance is the deeper signal.
- Choose Escape when the song treats partying, sex, intoxication, luxury, or fun mainly as release or distraction.
- Choose Status when wealth, fame, luxury, public success, or image is the central value.
- Choose Ego/Power when dominance, pride, superiority, swagger, or control is the central value.
- For therapy-style or confessional songs, ask: does the song actually move toward healing, or does it mainly display chaos? If chaos, choose Instability or Avoidance.
- For fame/wealth songs with depression, numbness, drugs, or chaos, consider Instability, Avoidance, Status, Escape, or Joy/Pleasure before Growth.
- For diss tracks or rivalries, consider Revenge or Ego/Power before Self-Worth.
